Griswold vs Connecticut

1965

Vote was 7-2 in favor of Griswold

The Comstock Laws

Federal laws passed in 1873

The following were banned from the Postal Service

  • Obscenity
  • Contraceptives
  • Anything sexual

Additional Laws Went Even Further

Made it illegal to distribute any information about or dealing with abortion, obscenity, or contraception

  • Some anatomy textbooks weren't sent to medical students as they were judged obscene

Objective of the laws

  • Make premarital sex more risky
  • Suppress vices in the world through denial
  • Protect the morals of the youth

Definition of Obscenity

Material encouraging or engaging in sexual activity without major importance

Justification

Corruption in the youth and the home is due to obscene literature

Obscenity may corrupt marriage as well as the power of the church

Mainly focused on priviledged families support

One Congressman's rationale was that without the risk of pregnancy, sex would seem like a harmless activity

The Case At Hand

Local leader of Planned Parenthood, Griswold, and one of its doctors were arrested under a state level Comstock law

Griswold loses once on local and then again on state level courts

Arguing for Griswold

Amendments in use:

  • 1st Amendment, right to assemble.
  • 3rd Amendment, consent before quartering soldiers.
  • 4th Amendment, right to security and privacy.

Though these rights are not found explicitly in the constitution, There is a clear implication.

Penumbras and Implications

I think penumbra is perhaps the worst word to describe this.

The reality is that the consitution is clearly predicated on philsophy not included within the document itself.

A Priori and Induction

What Justice Douglas meant by implication is induction.

The 4th and 5th amendment are clearly arrived at from a philosophy that is protective of an individual's privacy.

We are inducting the a priori of the consitution.
